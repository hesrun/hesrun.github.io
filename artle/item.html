ABOUT THIS WATCH

The present example is an extremely rare, spectacular and fine métiers d’art rose-gold second time zone wristwatch. It was produced in 2016 in a limited edition of 5 pieces for William and Son, at that time a Laurent Ferrier retail agent in London. The same design with a case in platinum 950 launched the same year, also as a limited edition of 5 pieces. Both are among the brand’s rarest watches.

Laurent Ferrier launched the Galet Classic Traveler collection with a second time zone indicator in 2013, equipping it with a sophisticated version of the in-house micro-rotor self-winding caliber. The Galet Classic Traveler retains the DNA of Laurent Ferrier’s inaugural Galet Double Spiral Tourbillon models. The shape of the Galet watch case is fully consistent with the English translation of this term, i.e., ‘pebble’, which means all components are smoothly rounded. This reflects the aesthetics of a classic hunting pocket watch. In particular, instead of using of a screwed caseback, the Galet watch is equipped with a press-fitted (snap-on) caseback, and to facilitate the opening of the case, there is a characteristic ‘lip’ on the rim of the caseback at the bottom, which further enhances the relationship between the design of Galet and the design of pocket watches. The case of the Galet watch is so carefully polished that the smooth, streamlined outlines are really emphasised. For this reason, the feeling of the watch on the wrist is very comfortable. Galet watches, including the present Galet Traveller, are among the most emotionally attractive watches of the small brands of today which specialize in the release of collector’s models. In this regard, Laurent Ferrier occupy one of the leading positions.

The ‘William & Son’ Edition has a métiers d’art dial with an artistic convex map of the world, which is made in blue tones using the champlevé fire enamel technique

A discreet design of the Laurent Ferrier Galet watch’s caseback with a sapphire crystal and a smooth polished rim, which usually lacks inscriptions and emblems, is one of the hallmarks of respectable luxury watches of Geneva origin. All technical information is as a rule placed as engraved and gilded inscriptions on the bridges of the caliber and as hallmarks on the back of the lugs. Laurent Ferrier follows this tradition, rarely allowing itself to deviate from it. This was, however, done as an exception in this special edition, with a William and Son logo engraved on the rim of the caseback.

The ‘William & Son’ Edition has a métiers d’art dial with an artistic convex map of the world, which is made in blue tones using the champlevé fire enamel technique on a plate of 18k white gold. The artist, in an effort to create an unusual attractive image, has painted the night lights of each region, and used gold-leaf to highlight the United Kingdom’s position on a globe.

The shape and finishing of the case are matched by a graceful, yet rather large and easy-to-use turban-shaped crown. The pin buckle follows the curve of the case, while the elongated markers and hands of the modified ‘spade & whip’ style, which are given soft streamlined shapes, give the watch a special charm and recognition. Hands of this design are best paired with a polished finish, which is what is most often done in the collection, including this watch.

Another attractive feature of this watch is the two pushers on the left-hand side of the case, shaped as traditional sliding switchers of a grande sonnerie pocket watch. Here they allow the hour hand to be adjusted forwards or backwards in one-hour increments. The date indicator is tied to a travel time, indicated by the central hour and minute hands, it is very convenient for the traveling owner of a watch.

 

MOVEMENT

The present watch features the self-winding micro-rotor caliber LF 230.1. It was designed and launched in 2013 as a dual-time complication version of the basic FBN 229.01 micro-rotor caliber.

Apart from the design with bridges and layout that is reminiscent of tourbillon chronometric calibers of the mid-20th century, the caliber LF 230.1 features an extremely rare type of escapement with double direct impulse transmission. This is very efficient, so that a single barrel is enough for a three-day power reserve. The prototype of such an escapement is the famous échappement naturel of Abraham-Louis Breguet, invented by him in 1789, which is implemented with state-of-the-art technologies using LIGA (nickel-phosphorous impulse wheels) and silicon DRIE (switching pallet-lever part).

Laurent Ferrier launched the Galet Classic Traveller collection with a second time zone indicator in 2013, equipping it with a sophisticated version of the in-house micro-rotor self-winding caliber.

The self-winding system uses a heavy 18k gold micro-rotor of unidirectional operation with a ratchet, and is mounted under the bridge on two ruby jewels.

The quality of the finish of the caliber can be described as very high and performed in the classic Genevan tradition. The outer surface of the bridges is decorated with a Côtes de Genève, vertically oriented and very sharply executed. The mainplate reveals perlage decoration, while the chamfers are bevelled and mirror polished with careful finishing of external and internal corners, which is a desirable high-end finish but often neglected by many other brands. The slots for screws, pins and jewels are chamfered and polished as well. The outer surfaces of the steel parts, including the screw heads, pawl, balance cock and micro-rotor bridge, are mirror-polished by hand. The inscriptions are engraved and gilded, and the micro-rotor is decorated with grain d’orge guilloché.

Brand story

The core of the 1941 Remontoire is the movement. From a technical perspective, it features an eight second remontoire, which serves to deliver a constant amount of power to the escapement using a secondary wheel, visible through the dial at 9 o’clock. Consisting of 258 individual components, the caliber features over a dozen different finishing techniques, from snailing to frosting. The bridges, shaped like the bell gable roofs of Dutch houses, are fashioned out of steel, which is much more difficult and costly to work with, than brass or silver.

Each individual bridge requires dozens of steps and up to ten hours of work, including mirror polishing of the screws, and chamfering rims and slots, demonstrating the brothers’ fascination with depth and light. The level of painstaking craftsmanship of the Remontoire movements is striking, and the movement is limited to only 188 pieces in total.

The case, measuring 39.5mm across and just 10.5mm thick, is made out of a 5N red gold alloy, known for its colour intensity and hardness. Its sculpted profile is executed with a specially shaped cutter, to create its characteristic hollowed, concave recesses and convex surfaces.

Different styles of finishing are featured throughout the three-level solid silver dial, which has the remontoire exposed at 9 o’clock. Every 8 seconds, the remontoire quickly spins and stops, delivering energy to the escapement. Every 8 seconds, the remontoire quickly spins and stops, delivering energy to the escapement.
